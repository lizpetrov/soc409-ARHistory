Nassau Hall

Nassau Hall is the oldest and most iconic building on Princeton University’s campus. For hundreds of years, students and staff have passed by this building. However, not all of these students and staff have been justly remembered as a part of Princeton’s history. While Princeton University Archives indicate that the first African American student to receive an A.B. was John Leroy Howard in 1947, Howard was not the first Black student to earn a degree from Princeton. There were a number of Black students, specifically graduate students, preceding Howard who earned advanced degrees in the 1800s. Further, there were many Black campus employees who played important roles at the University. Read on to hear some of their stories.

Nassau Hall has a complex history that is not mentioned below. Click these links to learn more about the ties of slavery to the fundraising for Nassau Hall, and slavery at the President's house (now the neighboring Maclean House).

Campus Employees

Alfred Scudder

Born free in 1840, Alfred N. C. Scudder was a janitor of Clio Hall, and his frequent inclusion in students’ photo albums suggests that he played a significant role in campus life. He may also have served as an assistant to Professors Stephen Alexander, despite the fact that Alexander believed white Americans to be superior to both Native and African Americans. He also may have worked with Professor John Schanck, likely assisting with scientific experiments and classroom instruction.

James Titus

James Titus was a free Black man who lived with his wife Margaret in Princeton’s Black neighborhood, the Witherspoon-Jackson community. He worked at Princeton as a messenger and bootblack, listed as a “College servant” in the 1860 census. He signed his name in graduating students’ autograph books, and was nicknamed “Navigator” by students as well.

James Johnson

James Johnson worked on Princeton’s campus for over 60 years and became a beloved figure to students and members of the Princeton community alike. To learn more about Johnson and the arch that was recently named in his honor, head over to the easternmost arch in East Pyne Hall.

James Simpson

James Simpson was a campus worker during the late nineteenth century. Little is known about his life or what he did.

Noah Long

Noah Long was a bootblack on campus. Little more is known about his life or time in Princeton.

Early Black Students

Daniel Wallace Culp

Daniel Wallace Culp was a student at Princeton Theological Seminary in the late 1870s. Though he was never an enrolled student at Princeton University, James McCosh allowed Culp to attend his psychology lectures at Princeton in 1876. This provoked some southern students to unenroll. The Princetonian reported on the incident, “five young gentlemen…recently left Princeton College rather than sit in the same room with a negro one hour a week…”.

Thomas McCants Stewart

Thomas McCants Stewart was born to free black parents in Charleston, South Carolina in 1852. After receiving a bachelor’s degree from the University of South Carolina at Columbia, he attended Princeton Theological Seminary from 1877 to 1880. During this time, he also enrolled in classes at Princeton, then the College of New Jersey. In 1882, James McCosh referred to Stewart as one of his former students of philosophy, though he did not graduate from the College of New Jersey.

Irwin Roundtree

Irwin William Langston “Dominie” Roundtree, one of Princeton’s earliest Black students, earned a Master of Arts from Princeton in 1895. Further, he may have been born into slavery, making him the only known previously enslaved person to have attended Princeton. Roundtree enrolled as an undergraduate at Lincoln University, the United States’ first degree-granting historically Black university, in 1882. He studied at Princeton Theological Seminary from 1892 to 1894. In 1904, Woodrow Wilson insisted that “no negro has ever applied for admission.” Wilson was a professor while Roundtree attended Princeton.

Abraham Parker Denny

Abraham Parker Denny was the first known Black student to receive a Master’s from Princeton when it was still the College of New Jersey in 1891. Denny served as superintendent for Princeton’s Witherspoon School for Colored Children and was an outspoken advocate against the nationwide lynching epidemic. The image above displays his name in the annually-published Princeton Catalog as an 1891 Doctors & Masters graduate.

James Monroe Boger

James Monroe Boger graduated from the College of New Jersey with a master’s degree in 1893, one of the first African Americans to do so. Afterward, he attended the Princeton Theological Seminary but passed away before completing his seminary degree. The Princeton University Archives have no files on Boger, but the image above displays his name in the annually-published Princeton Catalogue as an 1893 Doctors & Masters graduate.

Leonard Zachariah Johnson

Leonard Zachariah Johnson attended the Princeton Theological Seminary and received a master’s degree from Princeton in 1904. Woodrow Wilson was President of the University at the time. The image above is a graduate school record book entry proving Johnson’s degree.

George Shippen Stark

George Shippen Stark attended Princeton Theological Seminary and graduated Princeton with a master’s degree in 1906. Woodrow Wilson was President of the University at the time. The image above displays his name in the annually-published Princeton Catalog as a 1906 Doctors & Masters graduate.
